Addressing a misconception regarding the Uncertainty Principle

by Alfred Dahal

Posted on August 12, 2024

Almost everyone who has taken modern physics classes in high school has very likely come across the following inequality:

$$ \Delta\text{x }\Delta\text{p} \ge \hbar $$

This inequality is called Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle, and it is amongst the most important inequalities in all of physics. Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle states that both the position and the momentum of a particle cannot be measured with considerable accuracy simultaneously, and it is a direct consequence of the wave-particle duality. That means, the more accurately we measure the momentum of the particle, the more obscure its position becomes, and vice versa. But what if I told you that the notation above is actually quite a sloppy way of representing Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle?

To understand why, we first need to look at how the actual Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle looks like:

$$ \sigma_{x}\sigma_{p}\ge \hbar $$

Where and corresponds to the standard deviation of the data obtained from repeated measurements of the x and p of the particle.

The standard deviation is a measure of how dispersed or scattered the given data is, with respect to the mean. In the context of position and momentum, and gives us the respective measure of how scattered the values of the x and p are, with respect to their average values, or in quantum mechanical terms, the expectation values of the position and momentum. (Please do not get worked up with the names, they’re just terminologies that physicists use for simplicity’s sake.)

The real question is - what is the physical meaning of the standard deviation of x and p? The answer lies on what we are doing to the particle – we are taking repeated measurements of its position and momentum together. And somehow (specifically, by virtue of the wave-particle duality), the repeated measurements have ended up differing from one another, which leads to a non-zero standard deviation for both of the variables. (I would like to clarify that if we had obtained consistent values for the position and momentum after each measurement, the standard deviation of both the position and the momentum would’ve been zero – which is actually not what happens when measuring x and p of quantum particles, and this is an experimental result). So there is always a degree of uncertainty involved, when we are measuring the x and the p of the particle simultaneously and thus, we can come to the conclusion that the standard deviations and represent the uncertainties in the measurement of the position and the momentum of the particle respectively.

With this, we can conclude by saying that the delta notation for Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle is a very sloppy way of saying “uncertainty in the x and p”. The real way of expressing the uncertainties, is via the standard deviation of the x and the p.
